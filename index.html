How COVID-19 Carved Through 8,820 Lives in Massachusetts

Who it infected, how it attacked, and who it took

A data visualization of the first wave · Early 2020

Scroll

In 48 days, a single confirmed case became 8,820. Yet 96% of those infected survived. This is the story of the 4% who did not — and what separated them from everyone else.

What follows moves from the broadest view to the most intimate: the speed of the outbreak, the clinical funnel that sorted patients into survivors and victims, where it struck, who it killed, and finally, what the last days of a single patient looked like.

New Cases Exploded from a Single January Case to a March 8 Peak of 671 a Day

The first confirmed case appeared on January 20. By late February, new infections were doubling every few days. The surge peaked on March 8 at 671 cases in a single day, then gradually subsided over the following three weeks.

Daily new confirmed COVID-19 cases, Jan 20 – Mar 30, 2020. Source: Synthea 10K COVID-19 dataset.

Most Patients Recovered, but for 4% the Virus Proved Fatal

Most patients recovered at home. But for 11%, the virus triggered pneumonia — often progressing to respiratory failure, ARDS, or sepsis. In severe cases, it also attacked the heart, kidneys, and blood vessels. 357 patients died.

Each patient is classified by their most severe outcome. Slices are mutually exclusive and sum to 8,820.

Mortal Cases Appeared Across the Entire Commonwealth

No one county was immune: the virus reached every corner of Massachusetts. Mortality rates varied across counties, from 1.1% in Franklin County to 7.1% in Nantucket.

Shading indicates COVID-19 mortality rate by county. Hover over a county for case counts and deaths.

Whereas Mortality Remained Low Among Younger Patients, Older Patients Were at Much Higher Risk

Age was the single strongest predictor of death. Below age 50, fewer than 1 in 100 infected patients died. Above 80, it was closer to 1 in 4.

The outbreak peaked in under seven weeks. Most of the 8,820 infected walked away. But for the 21% who developed pneumonia, the virus opened a narrowing funnel — respiratory failure, organ collapse, death — that closed fastest on the old. Below age 50, fewer than 1 in 100 died. Above 80, it was closer to 1 in 4. The individual patient timelines make this pattern concrete: a predictable sequence of escalating clinical complications that hit the oldest patients with devastating efficiency.
